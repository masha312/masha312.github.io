Experiment: How I Increased My Strength By ~30% In Just 2 Weeks

Feb 3, 2020 | 4 min read

Some background

  • I’m 23, female, around 116lb and 18–19% body fat.
  • I began my working out journey about four years ago and have been consistently doing circuit strength training for about eight months.
  • I’ve been tracking my nutrition for about two years, with primary focus on protein and fat intake.

Problem

About a month ago I have noticed that with each workout, it became harder and harder for me to increase weights. Prior to that, I consistently kept on going up by about 2.5–7.5lb every 1–2 weeks. However, once I got up to using ~30lb for most upper body exercises, I began to struggle with going any heavier than that.

My weight also started climbing slowly — I went from 113.6lb weekly average to about 116.6lb, even though my diet remained the same.

Plan of action

To tackle my plateau, I thought it would be fun to run a small 30-day experiment. I decided to reevaluate my nutrition and see whether slightly altering my diet will set me back on track. I decided not to make any adjustments to my training program, because I wanted to ensure that changes (if there would be any) would only come as a result of changing my diet.

Here’s what my macros and nutrients looked like before the experiment:

  • Protein — 100g daily average (28%)
  • Fat — 33g daily average (21%)
  • Carbs — 178g daily average (51%)
  • Fiber — 38g daily average
  • Sodium — 1121mg daily average
  • Potassium — 1693mg daily average
  • Sugar — 111g daily average

Based on these, I planned to make just the following two adjustments:

  1. Increase protein by about 30g (to 130g daily average)
  2. Decrease sugar by as much as I possibly could

#2 deserves a special explanation. Even though I don’t eat sweets often, I am a huge fruit addict. Apples, oranges, grapes… I will have it all. I understand that fruit is much better for you than sweets but nevertheless, eating ten apples a day just can’t be good. So yeah, that’s where my ~100 grams of sugar per day came from.

For the next 30 days, I aimed to replace fruit with cucumbers, bell peppers, and other veggies. (I expected this to be very, VERY hard).

I estimated that my caloric intake will stay roughly the same, 1400–1700 per day, given that I will be eating less sugar (so extra protein will make up for less carbs).

Measuring progress

To test my progress, I decided to do the same workout at the beginning of the experiment, in the middle (2 weeks later), and at the end (4 weeks later).

Here’s the workout and my weights at the beginning:

Part 1 (4 rounds)

  • Shoulder press — 10 x 30lb
  • Bent shoulder raises — 20 x 20lb
  • Bicep curl pulses — 20 x 30lb
  • Bicep curl to shoulder press — 10 x 30lb

Part 2 (as many rounds as possible in 10 minutes — I did 5)

  • Squat to overhead press — 8 x 30lb
  • Decline pushup — 8
  • Lateral raise — 8 x 20lb

Results (so far)

To be honest, I didn’t expect to see any significant results only halfway into the experiment. However, when I repeated the workout two weeks later, I was quite amazed.

First though, here’s the rundown of my nutrition over these past two weeks, compared to the beginning of the experiment:

As you can see, I did manage to hit my two goals — upping my protein (although not as much as I intended to) and lowering down sugars (by a significant amount!). However, I also increased my sodium intake which I wasn’t happy about. Potassium levels also seem alarmingly low.

The surprising part was that using heavier dumbbells didn’t feel much harder. In fact, it felt just right — the same way that my old weights felt two weeks prior.

Also, I should note that I repeated the workout at exactly the same time of the day (early morning) and got about the same amount of sleep both nights. So, I think it’s safe to say that my progress came as a result of the diet adjustments :)

In addition to the gains, I also noticed a few other unexpected results:

  • My weight dropped a little bit from 116.6lb average to 115.4lb
  • My resting heart rate also dropped from 50 to 44
  • I started falling asleep much faster and I feel that I’m sleeping deeper

Final thoughts

While these changes worked for me, they may not work for everyone. There’s also evidence that high-protein diets may lead to certain health implications so it’s always best to consult a doctor before making any significant changes to your nutrition.

Given the above, this type of diet is not something that I plan on doing long term. After my experiment is over, I plan to lower protein and monitor my sodium intake better. Also, I really miss apples and want to bring them back into my diet — although in smaller amounts than before.🍎
